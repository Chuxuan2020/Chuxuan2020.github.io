About Me

Hello! My name is Chuxuan Li and I’m a Ph.D. candidate studying hydroclimate in the Department of Earth and Planetary Sciences at Northwestern University. I was born and grew up in southern China. I received my Bachelor’s degree in Marine Sciences from Sun Yat-sen University, a top 10 university in China, located in the beautiful coastal city of Guangdong Province. In 2014, my journey toward a B.A. took me to Taiwan as an exchange student where I spent half a year. The post-secondary education I received along with my living experiences in coastal areas stimulated my interests in the oceanic and atmospheric sciences. Therefore, I decided to go abroad to further these pursuits. That’s how I ended up in the U.S. Graduate studies at the University of North Carolina (UNC) at Chapel Hill provided me with the opportunity to explore, understand, and learn the details of atmospheric rivers (ARs) . From 2017 to 2020, I worked on the variability and predictability of AR activities over the North Atlantic. In two publications, we used high-resolution climate model outputs to investigate the modulation of large-scale climate modes on the occurrence frequency of ARs and the clustering behaviors of AR tracks and the induced extreme precipitation and winds. After I completed my Master’s degree at UNC, I joined Dr. Horton’s Climate Change Research Group (CCRG) in July 2020. Here, we study hydrological extremes and land surface hazards caused by ARs on the west coast of the U.S. using numerical modeling and remote sensing. In a 2022 publication, we assessed the debris-flow susceptibility for several wildfire burn scars from the 2020 wildfire season over a regional domain in central California. Ongoing research includes improving WRF-Hydro’s performance using state-of-the-science soil characteristic estimates and probabilistic forecast of hydro-geomorphic hazards. Ongoing collaborative research covers topics in urban flooding, landslide prediction, LIDAR-informed postfire debris flow retrospective simulations, soil moisture in compound hazards, and heat vulnerability.
