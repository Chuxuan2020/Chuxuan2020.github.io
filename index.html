About Me

Hello! My name is Chuxuan Li and I’m a Ph.D. candidate studying hydro-climate extreme events in the Department of Earth and Planetary Sciences at Northwestern University.

I was born and grew up in southern China. I received my Bachelor’s degree in Marine Sciences from Sun Yat-sen University, a top 10 university in China. In 2014, my journey toward a B.A. took me to Taiwan as an exchange student where I spent half a year. The post-secondary education I received along with my living experiences in coastal areas stimulated my interests in the oceanic and atmospheric sciences. Therefore, in 2017 I decided to go abroad to further these pursuits.

That’s how I ended up in the U.S. Graduate studies at the University of North Carolina (UNC) at Chapel Hill provided me with the opportunity to explore, understand, and learn the details of atmospheric rivers (ARs) . From 2017 to 2020, I worked on the variability and predictability of AR activities over the North Atlantic. In two publications, we used high-resolution climate model outputs to investigate the modulation of large-scale climate modes on the occurrence frequency of ARs and the clustering behaviors of AR tracks and the induced extreme precipitation and winds.

After I completed my Master’s degree at UNC, I joined Dr. Daniel Horton’s Climate Change Research Group (CCRG) in July 2020. Here, I use numerical models and remote sensing to study hydrological extremes and land surface hazards caused by ARs on the west coast of the U.S. In a 2022 publication, I assessed the debris-flow susceptibility for several wildfire burn scars from the 2020 wildfire season over a regional domain in central California. In another publication, I improved WRF-Hydro’s performance to simulate soil moisture and streamflow using state-of-the-science soil characteristic estimates. Ongoing research includes a probabilistic reforecast of hydro-geomorphic hazards over California. Ongoing collaborative research covers topics in urban flooding, landslide prediction, LIDAR-informed postfire debris flow retrospective simulations, soil moisture in compound hazards, and heat vulnerability.
